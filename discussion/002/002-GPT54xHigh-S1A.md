## Part A · Most-exciting version (POSITIVE pole)

The best version of Research Radar is not "a better alert system." It becomes a **research chief of staff** for a small AI lab: a **living map** of what changed this week, which changes matter to our 8-15 topics, what is probably real, and what deserves an experiment before the crowd notices.

In that world, the user's lived experience is unusually calm. Monday morning does not begin with forty tabs. It begins with one sharp brief: three genuinely new developments, two adjacent shifts worth watching, one overhyped claim, and one recommendation for where the lab should place a small bet. The aha moment is when the user realizes the system is not merely summarizing artifacts; it is **tracking the motion of ideas** across papers, code, institutions, and argument.

Why could this be 10x better than what exists? Because most tools stop at retrieval or summarization. A great Research Radar compounds discovery, interpretation, topic-memory, and taste-learning until the knowledge base becomes a **personalized institutional memory** rather than a pile of bookmarks. For a single operator leading a lab, that feels plausible: the topic set is bounded, feedback is frequent, and the budget is enough for an opinionated internal system before it is enough for a broad product.

## Part B · Most-damning version (NEGATIVE pole, independent)

Set the dream aside: the deepest risk is that this idea confuses information processing with judgment. The real bottleneck in frontier research is often not finding artifacts; it is deciding what to believe, what to ignore, and when to act. A system can ingest papers, blogs, repos, and discourse, yet still fail the only test that matters: does a strong human trust it enough to allocate scarce attention based on its output?

That is hard because "novelty," "impact," and "practicality" are not stable labels. They are delayed social judgments distorted by hype cycles, benchmark gaming, and selective demos. The radar may become eloquent without becoming reliable. Worse, it may create false confidence by overfitting to famous labs, GitHub acceleration, or whatever preferences it inferred from the user's past behavior. A taste-learning system for one operator could become a preference prison, narrowing curiosity instead of expanding it.

There is also a brutal engineering reality. Every input source is messy or fragile. Conference pages change. PDFs parse badly. Blogs are marketing-adjacent. Repos are noisy. Topic hierarchies rot. From memory, many tools have already attacked slices of this problem: alerts, paper recommenders, ranking sites, code leaderboards, literature digests. They help, but they do not become the durable brain of a lab. My honest advice to a founder would be: do not build the grand unified radar first. If you cannot win with a narrow weekly workflow that a real researcher would miss when it is gone, you probably should not build the full vision.

Claims I am fairly confident about:
- AI-research volume is already far beyond what one person can manually track well. Confidence source: broad exposure to the current paper/blog/repo firehose.

Claims that are still assumptions:
- A model can score novelty, impact, and practical usefulness accurately enough to earn long-term trust.
- A hierarchical topic graph can be updated automatically without drifting into taxonomy soup.
- Preference learning from one lab lead produces real "taste" rather than mere reinforcement of prior bias.
- The user wants a continuously updating system more than a disciplined weekly or twice-weekly cadence.

Known unknowns:
- Which failure hurts more in practice: missed important work or too many false positives.
- Whether frontier signal tends to appear first in papers, repos, blogs, or social discussion for the user's target topics.
- How often "reliable, forward-looking judgment" can be produced without human adversarial review.
