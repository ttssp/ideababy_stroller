## Part A · Adjacent directions

### 1. Lab Memory Theater
Reframe the radar from a personal research feed into the shared memory of a lab.

Instead of helping one person keep up, this becomes the place a whole group returns to when they ask, "How did our thinking on this topic change over six months?" New papers matter because they bend the lab's internal storyline: what we believed, what surprised us, what we quietly stopped believing. The aha is that frontier knowledge becomes a collective narrative, not just stored summaries.

### 2. Taste Apprenticeship Engine
Reframe the radar as a mentor for early-career researchers learning what "good taste" feels like.

A junior PhD or new RA does not mainly suffer from missing papers; they suffer from not yet knowing what deserves deep attention. This direction turns the system into an apprenticeship surface: "here are three works worth your Saturday, and here is why one is substance, one is polish, and one is noise." The aha is that the product is accelerated judgment, not accelerated reading.

### 3. Dissent Cartographer
Reframe the radar around finding disagreement, failed promises, and anti-hype signals.

Most systems chase what is rising. This one chases where the field is overclaiming or converging too fast. It maps counterarguments, negative results, edge cases, and quiet writing that punctures a fashionable story. The aha is that a lab may gain more from seeing cracks in consensus than from hearing the consensus earlier.

## Part B · Extended directions

### 1. Field Simulator
Push the idea to its most ambitious cut: a living map of where the field thinks it is going.

Every new work does not merely enter a database; it tilts the perceived future. The system keeps a moving picture of which bets are strengthening, which assumptions are decaying, and which questions are becoming newly legible. The feeling is less "I read the literature" and more "I can sense the weather of the frontier." The aha is that the object being maintained is the future-shape of a field.

### 2. One Good Page
Push the idea to its smallest viable emotional core: one brutally curated page per topic per week.

No grand knowledge base, just a ritual object people trust: one page saying what mattered, what did not, and what deserves another hour of thought. It wins by becoming the thing people actually read. The aha is that compression itself can be the moat; an overbuilt radar may be less loved than a single page with taste.

### 3. Studio Radar
Transplant the mechanism into an unrelated domain: a creative studio tracking aesthetic frontier shifts.

A game studio, fashion house, or film collective could use the same pattern to watch emerging motifs and visual moves across thousands of weak signals. The value would be similar: not raw discovery, but a living taste map that helps a team know what is derivative, what is ripening, and what feels crowded. The aha is that the proposal's deepest mechanism is not "paper tracking"; it is frontier sensemaking under abundance.

## Part C · Reframed directions

### 1. Conviction Coach
Question the premise that the core problem is information overload; maybe the real problem is weak conviction.

Researchers often do not need more input as much as they need help deciding what to believe, what to doubt, and what to ignore. In this framing, the system becomes a coach for intellectual commitment: what claims have earned trust, what areas deserve skepticism, and where your lab should stand slightly apart from the crowd. The aha is that selective belief may matter more than total awareness.

### 2. Question-First Scout
Question the solution shape: maybe the system should not start from incoming papers at all.

Instead, it begins from the lab's current questions, confusions, and half-formed theses, then watches the world through those lenses. New work only enters if it sharpens, breaks, or redirects a live question. The experience shifts from passive monitoring to active inquiry. The aha is that relevance is not a property of papers; it is a relationship between outside work and inside curiosity.

### 3. Boundary Object Maker
Question whether the deepest pain is "keeping up" rather than "thinking together across topics."

In a lab spanning 8 to 15 themes, the rarest thing may be shared conceptual language. This direction uses external research as raw material for creating common maps, metaphors, axes, and comparison frames so that people in neighboring areas can suddenly understand one another. The aha is that the system's highest value may be turning a multi-topic lab into a more coherent mind.
