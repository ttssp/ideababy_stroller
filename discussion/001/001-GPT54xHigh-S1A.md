## Part A · Most-exciting version (POSITIVE pole)
**The best version of Research Radar is not a feed reader. It is a private scientific intelligence system for one lab leader.** Instead of dumping links, it maintains a living map of what changed, why it matters, and what it collides with in the lab's current beliefs.

**The 10x moment is when the user stops "checking arXiv" and starts consulting a trusted radar before making research bets.** A great brief would say which work matters, which assumption it weakens, and what the team should inspect next.

**Why could this be dramatically better than today's tools? Because discovery, memory, and synthesis are still fragmented.** One tool alerts on papers, another tracks repos, another stores notes. A strong Research Radar fuses them into one loop: detect, compare, connect, remember, and update. Over time it becomes a compounding asset, and "state of the art" becomes an explicit, revisable map instead of a vague impression.

I believe this is possible because the signals are public, the user is narrow and opinionated, and current models are already good enough to extract structured summaries and draft useful comparisons when the workflow is tightly scoped. For one operator, this does not need universal truth; it needs a high hit rate on "things I would have regretted missing."

## Part B · Most-damning version (NEGATIVE pole, independent)
Set the dream aside. My honest skeptical view is that this idea may fail for a structural reason: **research importance is not sitting on the surface of documents waiting to be extracted.** The hard part is not collecting papers, blogs, and repos. The hard part is judgment: what is genuinely new, what is noise with strong marketing, what matters specifically to this lab, and what is merely adjacent. That judgment depends on tacit taste, local context, and long-running technical memory. LLMs can imitate that voice before they truly earn it.

There is also a classic knowledge-management trap here. The system promises a topic graph, lineage tracking, SOTA status, and proactive backfilling. In practice, ontologies drift, tags sprawl, and the knowledge base can become a graveyard of summaries nobody trusts for real decisions.

The notification layer is another danger. A radar that pushes "important work" must clear a very high bar. If it misses one crucial thing, trust drops. If it sends too many plausible-but-not-important things, trust also drops. The product could settle into a familiar bad equilibrium: expensive ingestion, impressive dashboards, low behavioral change.

If a founder asked me whether to build this, my truthful answer would be: not in the grand form described, and not with a one-week solo build target. I would worry that the result becomes a brittle pipeline plus polished summaries, while novelty detection, impact assessment, and knowledge-base coherence remain weakly solved. The right move might be a much narrower assistant for one person's workflow.

## Part C · Epistemic honesty — what I'd actually need to know
Claims I feel relatively confident about from memory:
- The volume of AI papers, repos, and blog posts is high enough that manual tracking does not scale for a busy lab lead.
- LLMs are often useful for first-pass summarization, clustering, and comparison when the domain and output format are constrained.
- GitHub stars, social buzz, and polished writing are noisy proxies for long-term research importance.

Claims that are mostly assumptions right now:
- A system can reliably infer novelty, impact, and practical usefulness well enough to guide research bets.
- A hierarchical topic ontology can stay coherent with limited manual curation.
- Cross-source linking between paper, repo, blog, and follow-on work is accurate enough to feel magical rather than messy.
- A solo operator can build a durable v1 in one week rather than a promising prototype.

Known unknowns:
- What prior products already tried to be a "research radar" for researchers rather than general news aggregation?
- Which parts of this problem users actually stick with: alerts, summaries, topic maps, or decision memos?
- How much full-text access, PDF parsing quality, and repo analysis quality is realistically available at low cost?
- Whether the user's real need is discovery, synthesis, or team-memory preservation.
